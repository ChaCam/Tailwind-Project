Arguments for and against American Imperialism

View More

Views on American imperialism were differentiated into two categories, for and against imperialism. Many places, even some that were not contiguous to the United States, almost became annexed. One such place was the Dominican Republic. President Grant believed in the annexation of the Dominican Republic, as seen through his proposed annexation of the state. This treaty proposed by Grant would not get past the Senate. One of the main opposing senators was Charles Sumner, who opposed the annexation as he claimed that it would become a violent and bloody state. This reason can be further supported by the fact that the government was not stable and people overthrew the country many times. In addition, due to the close proximity to the country of Haiti, people did not want to absorb the influence of the state. Most senators just wanted to keep the country by itself because of these reasons. The Dominican Republic was not the only state that was attempted to be annexed.


The Hawaiian islands would eventually be annexed by President William McKinley. However, before McKinley annexed it, the bill to annex the state was vetoed by the president at the time, Grover Cleveland. Cleveland believed that Hawaii was overthrown in an unfair way and he wanted to give them the same freedom that America had. Not only did he veto the annexation, but he also tried to restore the queen back to power. This anti-imperialistic nature of Cleveland would lead to the election of William McKinley who had opposing ideas of Cleveland. He heavily supported annexation, which is seen through the outcome of the Spanish-American War. He would, of course, annex the new Hawaiian islands. In addition, he would purchase the islands of the Philippines, Guam, and Puerto Rico. Some of these are even still part of the United States today.

The Spanish-American War

View More

Foreign policy also had a huge effect in the Spanish-American War. The president at this time was President McKinley, who was strongly in support of imperialism. He believed that Cuba should be independent from the Spanish, and that the United States owed protection to the territory. Also, he believed that the Philippines should become independent as well since they were being mistreated by Spain. Another cause of this war was the sinking of the United States' battleship, U.S.S. Maine, in Havana's harbor. Even though the ship exploded through an unknown means, the United States still believed that it was Spain who did it.


The war started after the United States deployed troops in Cuba. One of the main events of the war was the Battle of San Juan Heights, which the United States won. Another main battle was the Battle of Santiago de Cuba, which the United States also won. This led to the surrender at Santiago, Cuba. Spain officially signed the Treaty of Paris with the United States, ceding some of its territories. The outcome of this war led to the United States' acquisitions of many territories across the globe. These territories include Cuba, as discussed above, Puerto Rico, the Philippines, and the Sandwich Islands also known as Hawaii. McKinley wanted to keep these territories as part of the United States. He specifically said that he did not want to give the Philippines back to Spain because it would be dishonorable, give them to France or Germany because they were the United States' economic rivals, or leave them as an independent nation because they were not fit to govern themselves. Eventually though, the United States would lose two out of the four territories, which were Cuba and the Philippines. The Philippines is specifically important because it continues the theme throughout history of imperialism causing war.

American Imperialism

View More

American imperialistic ideas can be seen through the philosophies of Big Stick Diplomacy by Teddy Roosevelt and Dollar Diplomacy by William Howard Taft. The Big Stick Diplomacy can be best described as negotiating with the threat of a powerful military. It was partially influenced by the Monroe Doctrine to keep power centralized in the Americas. Roosevelt was like an international policeman between North and South America. This idea is used through the establishment of the Panama Canal. Roosevelt wanted this canal to be built to facilitate trade easily without having to go through the route around South America. Before it was built though, the United States led by Roosevelt recognized Panama as an official country after it had declared independence from Columbia. The president after Roosevelt, William Howard Taft, believed in a different policy called Dollar Diplomacy. Rather than Big Stick Diplomacy, it did not use strong military power, but instead used economic power and gave loans to foreign countries. Better relations wanted to be achieved with these countries, including Mexico, Nicaragua, and China.


There were also the Philippine islands that America had gained from the outcome of the Spanish-American War. American occupation of this territory did not last long, as the Philippine-American War occurred right after. The president at this time was William McKinley, who wanted to keep control of the new territories and believed in the idea of "The White Man's Burden" and wanted to assimilate the people of the islands. The American treatment of the Filippinos was not great to say the least. An example of the unethical treatment against these Filippinos can be seen through water cure where American soldiers would torture them by forcing them to drink a large amount of water. The territory would go on to last around thirty years until World War II.

WWI: From Neutrality to Intervention

View More

President Woodrow Wilson strongly believed in an idea called Moral Diplomacy. This idea meant that the United States supported other nations that had similar governments to their own. It helped the country pursue its economic interests throughout the world. It would eventually lead to the country's involvement in the Great War, later called World War I. This is evident through the fact that exports of the United States to the countries of Great Britain and France increased during World War I, and its exports to Germany increased after the war was over.


The United States' involvement in World War I was caused by many reasons. One of the main reasons was the Zimmerman Telegram. The message of this telegram stated that Germany shall form an alliance in Mexico if it entered war with Germany against the United States. President Wilson called for war in his message to Congress. His main reason that is stated in the message is that Germany kept attacking United States ships with their submarines in the Mediterranean. The main goal of the United States when joining the war was because of Wilson's strong belief in Moral Diplomacy. This meant that he and the country supported the Allies which included Britain and France, both countries with democratic governments. On the other hand, he and the Allies went against and did not support the Central Powers, including countries with monarchies such as Germany and the Ottoman Empire. Its role in World War I helped the Allies gain victory over the Central Powers. First of all, it helped by supplying the Allies with over a million troops. After all, the war was won by overpowering by force against the Central Powers. Another way they helped contribute to the war was creating a blockade on the German coast, heavily damaging their economy.

WWI: The Home Front

View More

World War I impacted American society in multiple ways. One way is that it increased the empowerment of women in society. Since the men were out fighting in war, women took over some of the traditional jobs of men as workers. These jobs included radio operators, messengers, and truck drivers. Some women even helped with the war effort, with many joining the Navy. However, there were also negative effects of the war on society. The war caused a very controversial law to be passed by Congress, called the Sedition Act of 1918. This act basically said that citizens could not express their opinions of the government in a negative way, or else punishment would occur. The law would eventually be repealed in 1920 because it violated the first amendment. There was also the Great Migration which was the period where millions of African Americans, some of which were poor and looking for better opportunities.


Another way World War I impacted American society was that it affected the economy of the United States, both positively and negatively. First of all, they used around thirty billion dollars to fund the war. This came from a variety of ways including taxes. The war would lead to a debt that would last for years. Again, there were also positive economic effects. The war helped facilitate trade between nations, specifically with the Allies. Later, the United States would also trade with former Central Powers such as Germany. The end of the war would also have a huge economical effect throughout the whole world. It made the United States a leading industrial power, as they could easily mass produce new goods efficiently. Some of these goods included automobiles, petroleum, and steel. In addition to being a leading producer, they also provided money to other countries who needed it.

The Aftermath of the War

View More

Before the Treaty of Versailles, President Woodrow Wilson released his Fourteen Points. These points included the freedom of the seas, peace, and adjustment of many colonial claims. The Treaty of Versailles used some of these points and was signed in 1919. The treaty was formally signed between Germany and the Allied Powers. With this treaty, Germany was at a loss in many things. Its many colonies in Africa and Asia were gone, its military was reduced, it had to pay war reparations to the Allies which would lead to their debt, and of course it formally accepted defeat.


After the war ended, there were efforts to establish the League of Nations. It was heavily pushed by the president at the time, Woodrow Wilson. The League would help unite the countries such as Belgium, France, England, and Italy. The United States also did not want every country to join, such as Germany and Russia because of Wilson's idea of moral diplomacy since they had different forms of government. The league would not be able to stay together without the support of the United States. However, not all people of the United States supported the establishment of this league. One such person was Henry Cabot Lodge, a Senator at the time who had opposing ideals to Wilson. He claimed that the United States should not get into the issues of the other countries in Europe. In addition, he did not want to fight for or against other countries. These would be the main ideas of the establishment of the League of Nations. Even though the United States heavily advocated the league, they never joined. This is one of the many reasons that the League of Nations was a failure, as it was replaced with the United Nations in the next twenty-five years.
